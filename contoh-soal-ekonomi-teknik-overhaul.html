  • 17. Basic Principles of Islamic Economics Islamic economics is presented as a superior alternative to existing economic doctrines and systems. It proposes a certain set of moral and institutional reforms based on the traditional sources of Islam and these reforms are designed to overhaul the existing economic systems. Islamic economics emphasizes the encouragement of communal, non-individualistic values and fighting against selfishness. This is one of the distinguishing aspects of Islamic economics for it views communal values as critical to an economic system’s operation. Economics has been important in every civilization, and as part of man’s activity, deals with the production of goods, accumulation of wealth, labor, work, trade, and exchange of goods and services and so on. Economics was never considered as a separate discipline or a distinct domain of activity in Muslim societies like their Western counterparts. Consequently, there is no even a word for economics in classical Arabic. The term Iqtisad (economic) is used as a fair translation of the modern term “economics” in Arabic. Iqtisad has a very different meaning in classical Arabic. Ihya-u-Ulum-id-Din, a famous book written by Imam Gazzali defines the term primarily as a principle of moderation (i.e., golden mean) where it means “neither too much nor too little”. Early contributions to Islamic economics mostly came from Pakistanis, after the initial writings of Maududi, a famous Islamic activist. By 1975, about 75 percent of the contributions to Islamic economics are by Pakistanis. The share of non-Pakistani contributors has grown since then, but that of South Asians continues to dominate. Iran follows Pakistan in putting Islamic economics into practice. Therefore, it is reasonable to accept that these two countries made substantial contributions to Islamic economics. According to Mohammed B. Sadr, three basic principles of Islamic economy are multi-faceted ownership, economic freedom within a certain limit, and social justice. These are the three basic components of Islamic economic doctrine, according to which its theoretical content is defined. Islamic economics can also be distinguished from other economic regimes in terms of the broad lines given by these components: 1. The principle of multi-faceted ownership Ownership is accepted in a variety of forms instead of only one kind of ownership such as private ownership, public ownership, and state ownership. So, it adopts the principle of multi-faceted ownership. Islamic economic doctrine essentially differs from capitalist and socialist regimes with this respect. It allows private ownership for a number of kinds of property and means of production. Similarly, it allows public ownership and state ownership for some kinds of wealth and property. But in its view neither the private ownership nor the public ownership is the basic rule. For this reason, it is very difficult to call it as capitalist economy or a socialist economy. Individual ownership, state ownership, and public ownership are three parallel forms in Islamic law (Shari‟ ah). The Holy Book Qur‟an makes clear that everything in the universe belongs to God and He is the real owner of everything: “Whatever in the heavens and whatever is in the earth belongs to Allah” [Al-Baqarah, 2: 284]. “And Allah is in the kingdom of heavens and the earth, and Allah has power over everything” [Al-Imran, 3: 189]. Real ownership belongs to God and man holds property in trust for which he is accountable to him, in accordance with the rules clearly defined by the Shari‟ ah. Therefore, absolute ownership of man is an alien concept in Islam, as it belongs to God alone. Accordingly, the acquisition of property as well as its use and disposal are subject to limits set and principles guided by the norms of Islamic teaching. There are definite obligations towards others attending upon the individual ownership rights. The respective scopes of the three kinds of ownership are not strictly defined but left to be determined in the light of certain principles, depending on the needs and circumstances.

6. Suatu perusahaan merencanakan pengembangan usaha dengan investasi baru senilai 1.200 jt. Dengan perkiraan pendapatan sebesar 400jt per tahun dari tahun ke 2 hingga ke 7, setelah itu menurun secara gradiend sebesar 15jt per tahun. Biaya operasional yang dikeluarkan sebesar 50jt per tahun dan akan meningkat secara gradient sebesar 10jt per tahun. Umur investasi di prediksi 12 tahun dengan nilai sisa 500jt. Terdapat pendapatan lump-sum sebesar 300jt pada tahun ke 6, dan terdapat biaya overhaul sebesar 100jt pada tahun ke 7. Bila tingkat suku bunga diasumsikan sebesar 10% Pertanyaan : 1. Apakah investasi ini layak untuk dijalankan ? Berikan penjelasan dengan menggunakan perhitungan analisa Annual Worth. 2. Gambarkan diagram alir nya dengan horizon waktu selama 12 tahun.​


Untuk menentukan apakah investasi ini layak atau tidak, kita dapat menggunakan analisis Annual Worth atau Nilai Tahunan. Dalam analisis ini, kita akan menghitung selisih antara arus kas masuk dan keluar pada setiap tahun, dengan menggunakan tingkat suku bunga sebagai faktor pengdiskontonya.

1. Perhitungan analisis Annual Worth:
- Pendapatan per tahun:
   - Tahun 2-7: 400jt
   - Tahun 8-12: 400jt - (15jt x (tahun-7))
- Biaya operasional per tahun: 50jt + (10jt x (tahun-1))
- Biaya overhaul tahun ke 7: -100jt
- Pendapatan lump-sum tahun ke 6: 300jt
- Nilai sisa di akhir tahun ke 12: 500jt

Untuk menghitung nilai sekarang dari setiap arus kas, kita menggunakan rumus:
PV = CF / (1+r)^t
dimana PV adalah Present Value (Nilai Sekarang), CF adalah Cash Flow (arus kas), r adalah tingkat suku bunga, dan t adalah tahun.

Menggunakan tingkat suku bunga 10%, kita dapat menghitung nilai sekarang dari setiap arus kas pada tahun yang bersangkutan:

- Nilai sekarang pendapatan tahun 2-7:
PV pendapatan = Σ (PV pendapatan per tahun) dari tahun 2 hingga 7
           = Σ (400jt / (1+0.1)^t) dari tahun 2 hingga 7
           = (400jt / (1+0.1)^2) + (400jt / (1+0.1)^3) + ... + (400jt / (1+0.1)^7)

- Nilai sekarang biaya operasional tahun 2-12:
PV biaya = Σ (PV biaya operasional per tahun) dari tahun 2 hingga 12
        = Σ ((50jt + (10jt x (tahun-1))) / (1+0.1)^t) dari tahun 2 hingga 12
        = ((50jt + (10jt x (2-1))) / (1+0.1)^2) + ((50jt + (10jt x (3-1))) / (1+0.1)^3) + ... + ((50jt + (10jt x (12-1))) / (1+0.1)^12)

PV overhaul = (100jt / (1+0.1)^7)

PV nilai sisa = (500jt / (1+0.1)^12)

17. Basic Principles of Islamic Economics Islamic economics is presented as a superior alternative to existing economic doctrines and systems. It proposes a certain set of moral and institutional reforms based on the traditional sources of Islam and these reforms are designed to overhaul the existing economic systems. Islamic economics emphasizes the encouragement of communal, non-individualistic values and fighting against selfishness. This is one of the distinguishing aspects of Islamic economics for it views communal values as critical to an economic system’s operation. Economics has been important in every civilization, and as part of man’s activity, deals with the production of goods, accumulation of wealth, labor, work, trade, and exchange of goods and services and so on. Economics was never considered as a separate discipline or a distinct domain of activity in Muslim societies like their Western counterparts. Consequently, there is no even a word for economics in classical Arabic. The term Iqtisad (economic) is used as a fair translation of the modern term “economics” in Arabic. Iqtisad has a very different meaning in classical Arabic. Ihya-u-Ulum-id-Din, a famous book written by Imam Gazzali defines the term primarily as a principle of moderation (i.e., golden mean) where it means “neither too much nor too little”. Early contributions to Islamic economics mostly came from Pakistanis, after the initial writings of Maududi, a famous Islamic activist. By 1975, about 75 percent of the contributions to Islamic economics are by Pakistanis. The share of non-Pakistani contributors has grown since then, but that of South Asians continues to dominate. Iran follows Pakistan in putting Islamic economics into practice. Therefore, it is reasonable to accept that these two countries made substantial contributions to Islamic economics. According to Mohammed B. Sadr, three basic principles of Islamic economy are multi-faceted ownership, economic freedom within a certain limit, and social justice. These are the three basic components of Islamic economic doctrine, according to which its theoretical content is defined. Islamic economics can also be distinguished from other economic regimes in terms of the broad lines given by these components: 1. The principle of multi-faceted ownership Ownership is accepted in a variety of forms instead of only one kind of ownership such as private ownership, public ownership, and state ownership. So, it adopts the principle of multi-faceted ownership. Islamic economic doctrine essentially differs from capitalist and socialist regimes with this respect. It allows private ownership for a number of kinds of property and means of production. Similarly, it allows public ownership and state ownership for some kinds of wealth and property. But in its view neither the private ownership nor the public ownership is the basic rule. For this reason, it is very difficult to call it as capitalist economy or a socialist economy. Individual ownership, state ownership, and public ownership are three parallel forms in Islamic law (Shari‟ ah). The Holy Book Qur‟an makes clear that everything in the universe belongs to God and He is the real owner of everything: “Whatever in the heavens and whatever is in the earth belongs to Allah” [Al-Baqarah, 2: 284]. “And Allah is in the kingdom of heavens and the earth, and Allah has power over everything” [Al-Imran, 3: 189]. Real ownership belongs to God and man holds property in trust for which he is accountable to him, in accordance with the rules clearly defined by the Shari‟ ah. Therefore, absolute ownership of man is an alien concept in Islam, as it belongs to God alone. Accordingly, the acquisition of property as well as its use and disposal are subject to limits set and principles guided by the norms of Islamic teaching. There are definite obligations towards others attending upon the individual ownership rights. The respective scopes of the three kinds of ownership are not strictly defined but left to be determined in the light of certain principles, depending on the needs and circumstances.
